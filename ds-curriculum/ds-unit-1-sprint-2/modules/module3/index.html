Module 3: Bayesian Statistics

So far, all statistics we've studied are frequentist statistics - drawing conclusions about an entire population or process by generalizing from the frequency or proportion of observed data. Frequentist statistics is well established and is still the default in most situations - but Bayesian statistics is an increasingly popular approach and an arguably better model for our brains and how we form and update beliefs based on evidence.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

  • Understand conditional probability notation and be able to apply it
  • Compare and contrast frequentist and Bayesian approaches to inference
  • Use Bayes' theorem to update a prior probability iteratively

Interactive Exploration

Bayesian Thinking in Practice

To better understand Bayesian thinking, consider this classic example:

A medical test for a disease is 99% accurate, meaning it correctly identifies 99% of sick people as sick and 99% of healthy people as healthy. The disease affects 1% of the population. If you test positive, what is the probability you actually have the disease?

Many people intuitively answer 99%, but using Bayes' theorem reveals that the answer is much lower. This demonstrates the power of Bayesian thinking and how it can contradict our intuitions.

Try calculating this yourself using Bayes' theorem!
